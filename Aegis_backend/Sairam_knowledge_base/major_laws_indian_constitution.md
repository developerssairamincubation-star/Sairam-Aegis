# Major Laws of the Indian Constitution

> **Note:** India's legal framework is rooted in the **Constitution of India (1950)**, the supreme law of the land. Below is a structured overview of the major constitutional provisions, fundamental laws, and landmark legislation that govern the Republic of India.

---

## 1. The Constitution of India (1950)

The Constitution came into force on **26 January 1950**. It is the longest written constitution in the world, comprising:

- **Preamble** — Declares India a Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic Republic
- **22 Parts** and **448 Articles** (as amended)
- **12 Schedules**
- **106 Amendments** (as of 2024)

---

## 2. Fundamental Rights (Part III — Articles 12–35)

|---------|-------|

---

## 3. Directive Principles of State Policy (Part IV — Articles 36–51)

Non-justiciable but fundamental to governance. Key principles include:

- **Art. 39** — Equal pay for equal work; right to adequate means of livelihood
- **Art. 41** — Right to work, education, and public assistance
- **Art. 44** — Uniform Civil Code
- **Art. 45** — Early childhood care and education
- **Art. 46** — Promotion of educational and economic interests of SC/ST
- **Art. 48** — Organisation of agriculture and animal husbandry
- **Art. 51** — Promotion of international peace and security

---

## 4. Fundamental Duties (Part IVA — Article 51A)

Added by the **42nd Amendment, 1976** (originally 10 duties; 11th added in 2002):

- Abide by the Constitution and respect national symbols
- Cherish ideals of the freedom struggle
- Uphold sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India
- Defend the country and render national service
- Promote harmony and brotherhood
- Preserve the composite culture and heritage
- Protect the natural environment
- Develop scientific temper and spirit of inquiry
- Safeguard public property
- Strive for excellence in all spheres
- Provide education to children aged 6–14 years

---

## 5. Union and Its Territory (Part I — Articles 1–4)

- **Art. 1** — India, that is Bharat, shall be a Union of States
- **Art. 3** — Parliament's power to form new states, alter boundaries, or change names

---

## 6. Distribution of Powers

### Union List (List I) — 100 subjects
Defence, foreign affairs, atomic energy, railways, banking, currency, etc.

### State List (List II) — 61 subjects
Public order, police, agriculture, land, local government, etc.

### Concurrent List (List III) — 52 subjects
Education, forests, trade unions, marriage, adoption, succession, etc.

> In case of conflict between Union and State laws on Concurrent List subjects, **Union law prevails** (Art. 254).

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| Art. 124–147 | Supreme Court of India |
| Art. 214–231 | High Courts in States |
| Art. 233–237 | Subordinate Courts |
| Art. 226 | High Court's power to issue writs |
| Art. 32 | Supreme Court's writ jurisdiction (Fundamental Rights) |
| Art. 137 | Review of SC judgements |
| Art. 141 | SC decisions binding on all courts |

### Five Types of Constitutional Writs (Art. 32 & 226)
1. **Habeas Corpus** — Release of illegally detained person
2. **Mandamus** — Direction to perform a public duty
3. **Prohibition** — Prevent inferior courts from exceeding jurisdiction
4. **Certiorari** — Quash orders of inferior courts/tribunals
5. **Quo Warranto** — Challenge to a person's right to hold public office

## 13. Key Doctrines Evolved by Indian Courts

- **Basic Structure Doctrine** — *Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)* — Parliament cannot amend the basic structure of the Constitution
- **Doctrine of Severability** — Unconstitutional part of a law is severed; rest remains valid
- **Doctrine of Eclipse** — Pre-constitution laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights become dormant
- **Reasonable Classification** — Art. 14 permits classification if it has a rational nexus to the object
- **Proportionality** — Restriction on rights must be proportionate to the objective
